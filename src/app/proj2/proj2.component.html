II. Writing Is Compulsory

In my third year of college I took an Advanced Writing course, and I wrote the following as a response to the prompt Reflect upon a past work of your writing:

From an early age, it was ingrained in me, and probably many others, that for a student to choose to be an English major, they have to want to be an English major. Perhaps, with this notion lurking in the back of my mind throughout my college life, my disdain, and even hatred, for writing snowballed into something greater…. Struggling at a practice that is such a fundamental part of my choice of study felt like a blaring sign that I had made a mistake in my decision.

Upon re-reading this piece, I paused on this particular section. I had not felt this way about writing in a long time. This work of writing came with my frustrations with pedagogical practices surrounding writing. We read similar moments in our reading of Joyce and Lorde, and, in spite of the failures of their respective educational institutions, writing remained their path, their inevitable calling.

When I write, I get frustrated, and when I am frustrated, I write.

Vita Saxville-West's writing was no good in Woolf's eyes, and yet Orlando is seen as a love letter for Vita, perhaps one of the grandest expressions of love to exist. “The Oak Tree,” the draft Orlando found herself returning to, the physical pages which would randomly reappear throughout her journey, had to signal something. Telling her story is essential to Orlando. For Orlando to write she must live. Woolf wrote Orlando out of love. To write is to love.

In thinking about the conflict between the pedagogy of writing and my consequent disdain for writing in general, I resolved to write with a new perspective.
