IV. Stories That Made Me Cry

When I was a child, my mother would read to me children’s books before bedtime. A book I would often pick was Ruby’s Wish, purely for the amusement I found in my mother’s reaction to it. Every single time she read this book, she would burst into tears.

Ruby’s Wish is the tale of a young girl in China who wished to go to college. For the longest time it was assumed she would be married off instead of being able to pursue her dreams. The book ends with a heartfelt conversation, where Ruby is granted a spot at a university (Bridges).

At the time, I didn’t understand why this story made my mother so emotional. Perhaps the role of housewife was so outside the scope of my imagination. I did play house with my friends as a child, but I suppose I didn’t yet understand how some could view the domestic role as a hindrance to the pursuit of other paths, other dreams.

In my Creative Writing course we read Ken Liu’s award-winning short story “The Paper Menagerie.” While categorized as a work of fiction, Liu’s story spoke of a cultural disconnect between a mother and her mixed-race son. The tale places a twist of magic on a typical story of assimilation. The narrator’s mother passes away, and he stumbles across a scrawled note to him in one of the origami animals his mother would make for him.

Son, I know that you do not like your Chinese eyes, which are my eyes. I know that you do not like your Chinese hair, which is my hair. But can you understand how much joy your very existence brought to me? And can you understand how it felt when you stopped talking to me and won’t let me talk to you in Chinese? I felt I was losing everything all over again.

Why won’t you talk to me, son? The pain makes it hard to write (Liu 9).

I saw this idea recur in our reading of Audre Lorde’s Zami. For my in-class presentation, I selected the following excerpt from the biomythography:

When I moved out of my mother’s house, shaky and determined, I began to seek some different relationship to this country of our sojourn. I began to seek some more fruitful return than simple bitterness from this place of my mother’s exile, whose streets I came to learn better than my mother had ever learned them. But thanks to what she did know and could teach me, I survived in them better than I could have imagined. I made an adolescent’s wild and powerful commitment to battling in my own full eye, closer to my own strength, which was after all not so very different from my mother’s. And there I found other women who sustained me and from whom I learned other loving. How to cook the foods I had never tasted in my mother’s house. How to drive a stick-shift car. How to loosen up and not be lost (Lorde 104).

I began to see a pattern – children of immigrants pausing to question their identities only in light of their separation from them, children growing to appreciate their parents only when they are gone. This made me look into my own relationships and my own concerns with my heritage and my identity. I pondered my grandfather, the stoic figure who’s past I realized I knew nothing about:

Man From Seoul

lays in bed, watching TV.


A cigarette teetering on

the edge of the nightstand.


I am 12, standing in

the doorway, staring


quietly, at his stillness. The wrinkled

hands. The glossy eyes that look Elsewhere.


And for a moment, I see him--

Gunshots ring over the


chatter of the TV. Ripe wound above the knee.

DMZ stories I could not comprehend.


His head turns, speaking

in a tongue I was meant for, but


never learned. I only walk to him when

he beckons me with a wrinkled finger–


I could not comprehend.

He speaks. The TV blares.
